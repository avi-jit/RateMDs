I write this not only as an evaluation of Dr Kalimi but as a patient's guide to those facing a similar health concern – which is an advanced but asymptomatic aortic stenosis condition which must be corrected asap with valve replacement. When you are given this diagnosis by a top-notch cardiologist you trust who recommends his choice of cardiothoracic surgeon to perform your aortic valve replacement, you usually don't shop around. But, having a Harvard trained cardiologist brother-in-law as I do, you automatically get a second opinion. And the verdict: “You have no option and Kalimi is one of the best – so go for it and do what he says!” Having done some online research, I learned about the TAVR implant and thought, hey, this would be a walk in the park. But after discussing my options with Kalimi, he recommended that I forego the “easy out” TAVR implant and opt for the “gold standard” of open heart valve replacement. He does both procedures but feels that if the patient is in good health and can tolerate the surgery (which apparently I am) outcomes are better with less risk of near or long term complications with the added advantage that the replacement valve would be organic (bovine tissue) and not mechanical so rejection would not be an issue and a long term blood thinner regimen would be avoided. He also reassured me that his version of “open heart” would be minimally invasive. On the latter point, I am in no position to judge what qualifies as “minimally invasive” but based on the feedback I got from my cardiologist brother-in-law who has attended many post-op open heart surgical patients, my quick and virtually pain-free recovery was “astonishing”. Anecdotally, I jokingly asked Dr. Kalimi if he would save my wornout valve in a jar of alcohol as a souvenir to display on my mantel. He laughed and, while declining my request, said he would go me one better – producing a very high quality color before-and-after photograph of the interior of my open heart with the gnarly old valve and the new bovine valve neatly stitched in. I passed the photo along to my brother-in-law who in turn showed it to some colleagues who universally pronounced it “beautiful work”. Now in my fourth post-op month with 12 weeks of cardio rehab under my belt, I feel physically rejuvenated and grateful to have lucked into the hands of such a skilled and careful (and caring) medical practitioner. And while I'm at it, I have only high praise for the skill, attentiveness and care shown to me and my family by the entire staff at Southside Hospital.
